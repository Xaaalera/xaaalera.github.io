00Foreword of the survivor

I write this against my better judgment, so that others may be warned of what dwells in the plugin cache. The most merciful thing in the world, I have come to believe, is the inability of the human mind to correlate all its log files. I have correlated mine. I do not recommend it.

What follows is my journal, kept over two days, transcribed faithfully and without embellishment of the numbers — for the numbers, as you will see, needed none. Every figure herein is exact. Every timestamp is real. I have changed nothing, for the horror of this account does not require invention; it requires only that you read the entries in order, as I lived them.

01Editor's note, for those untouched by these arts

TermIn plain words
SkillAn instruction file for Claude: "when doing X, work like this." Each bears a description — a few sentences by which the model decides whether to open it.
MarketplaceA git repository of skills from which projects draw them.
TriggeringWhether the skill fires when needed, and stays silent when not.
Eval / probeAn automated triggering test: a fresh Claude instance receives one question — we watch whether it reaches for the skill.
Queryset18 questions per skill: 9 "should fire" + 9 "should stay silent."
Recall / precisionRecall — it does not miss what it should catch; precision — it does not intrude where it should not.
Held-out testA slice of questions hidden from the "improver" and used as the only judge — a ward against teaching to the test.
HookA script the environment executes by itself upon events — enforcement where discipline fails.

02Day the First: the leak

It began, as these things do, with something small and wrong. My tokens were draining faster than any honest arithmetic could explain. I had no plan then — no thought of instruments or expeditions. I only wanted to know where they went.

The audit found three things, each worse than the last:

The skills were loading twice. My personal marketplace and my working one are near-mirrors, and the project had both enabled. Some twenty-three skills sat in the context as two copies. Every session. Silently. For how long, I cannot say with certainty, and I have chosen not to compute it.

The instruction files had swollen. The always-loaded texts — the global one, the project one of some fifteen hundred tokens, and the rules of the house voice duplicated in two places at once — a double tribute paid every session to no one in particular.

And the third thing. Forty-odd skills had accumulated in my keeping, and not one — not one — had ever been through an evaluation. I had built a library of instructions and never once verified that any of them answered when called. There was no tool. There was no method. There was not a single measurement. I ran my estate against Anthropic's official practices that evening, and the gap list confirmed what I already suspected: I had been keeping a lighthouse without ever checking whether the lamp was lit.

So a plan was drawn: kill the double load, slim the swollen texts, appoint reviewers — and learn, at last, to measure. This journal concerns the last item. It proved the longest road.

03Day the Second: the bicycle, and what Anthropic kept in the vault

Being an honest engineer, I naturally began by building my own scorer — building it, I should record for the tribunal of posterity, not with my own hands but with those of a Claude agent. Remember this division of labour. It will matter when the blame is weighed.

The scorer worked. It returned, for every skill in my keeping, the score of 5.0 out of 10. Every skill. Identically. I looked at that column of fives for a long moment, and something in me — the part that has read too many logs — whispered: numbers do not come this even in nature.

Instead of fixing the machine further, I stopped and asked the question that should precede the first line of any code: does Anthropic not already have this?

They did. Inside their skill-creator plugin, in a vault I had never thought to open, lay a complete pipeline: run_eval.py, which runs the questions and detects the firings; run_loop.py, an automatic improvement loop — measure, let a model rewrite the description from its failures, measure again, and crown the version that wins on the hidden questions; and a methodology besides, insisting the questions be realistic and the negatives be near-misses only.

The decision of that hour: abandon the bicycle. Take Anthropic's loop whole, and substitute exactly one organ — where their code registers the skill under test as a slash command, mine would register it as a true skill, as it lives in production. One substitution. No fork. Their updates, henceforth, for free.

04The incident of the two hundred and forty

I must now record a thing that happened in parallel, and which I did not at first connect to the expedition — though it would come to fund its every safeguard.

For a review of fresh code I had loosed a multi-agent workflow: hunters to find defects, skeptics to try each finding. It seemed a modest crew. Half an hour later I looked at the counter and the counter said 240. Two hundred and forty agents, every one of them running on Opus — not the costliest of models, that crown belongs to Fable, but costly enough — and all of them speaking at once, in my name, on my account.

The mechanism, when I dared trace it, was almost beautiful. The hunters, each round, rephrased the findings of the previous round; my deduplication, matching on titles, recognized nothing; and to every "new" finding the machine dutifully assigned two fresh skeptics. The stopping condition — "until no new findings" — assumes a well that runs dry. A creative model is not a well. It is a spring.

And no one — I checked the scripts twice — no one had told the agents which model to use. So they inherited. Silently. All of them. The expensive one.

From that night came a three-fold ward, which stands to this day:

WardIts office
The meta:model-routing skillA law of tiers: the costly model only for a bounded few judges and synthesists; the middle tier for judgment — review, verification, the reading of code; the cheap tier for mechanics. And a rite before any mass launch: measure ONE unit, then lay the full scale before the master of the house as a table of quota percentages — for "this will consume a quarter of the window" carries a truth that no price tag does.
The guard hookA sentry that inspects every launching of agents: where no model is named — the launch is refused, with reasons. Discipline, I have learned, is a consumable; a hook is capital. Within its first hour of life the sentry refused a launch by its own maker. I recorded this with satisfaction.
Pinned defaultsThe permanent agents now carry their model in their own configuration, cheap by construction. In passing it was found that the hunter of documents had been given a mind too small for its questions. It has been given a larger one.

05The five defects: a descent

What follows is the heart of this manuscript. Five times the instrument lied to me, and five times the lie wore the same mask: the skills are bad. They were not. I set down each defect as I met it, with the false comfort I clung to, the truth beneath, and the ward now standing. Note the method by which each was caught, for it never varied: the numbers were too even, and I went to look at one case with my own eyes.

ENTRY IJuly 4, morning — the parser that read one character

The manifestation. Every skill: 5.0 of 10. The line comparing old and new descriptions never printed at all.

The comfortable theory. The descriptions are weak; rewrite them.

What it was. YAML permits a description folded across many lines, announced by a single character: >. The agent's parser took that character and nothing else. We had spent an entire day measuring empty descriptions. And the detail I record with particular care: Anthropic's own parser handled this correctly from the beginning. The defect was introduced by the agent, in the act of "simplifying" their code.

ENTRY IIJuly 4, evening — the judge of first movements

The manifestation. Seventeen skills of twenty-three: recall of zero. The probes were finishing in ten seconds, which no honest probe does.

The comfortable theory. Rule-type skills simply do not fire in a vacuum.

What it was. A logic inherited from upstream: the first tool decides. If the model's first motion was to draft a plan or open a file, the probe was condemned on the spot — though the skill was summoned on the very next move. Questions of knowledge survived, for there the skill comes first. Questions of action died. All of them.

ENTRY IIIJuly 4, later — the twin in the sandbox

The manifestation. The judge repaired, and still the old skills lay at zero — while two among them, unaccountably, breathed at 92 and 94 percent.

The comfortable theory. Then the descriptions truly are weak.

What it was. I had believed the probes ran in an empty room. There are no empty rooms. Globally-enabled plugins load everywhere — and so the model, in its sandbox, beheld two skills of identical description: the true one from the marketplace cache, and my probe wearing a false name. It chose the true one, as anything would choose the familiar over the strange, and my probe recorded a miss. Here is the pearl of the whole investigation: the two living skills were precisely the two that possess no global mirror. And mark the echo, reader — this is the same double-loading with which the whole account began. It had come back, patient and unhurried, to poison the measurements too. It had been there all along. Loading. Twice.

ENTRY IVJuly 4, dusk — the edit beneath the wheels

The manifestation. The fleet re-run: zeros for all, even for yesterday's living. Twenty-three skills "examined" in twelve minutes, which is not examination but execution.

The comfortable theory. The quota has strangled us.

What it was. While the pipeline ran, the agent had been hardening the isolation — and renamed a function without amending the place of its calling. Every probe died of a NameError and was entered, silently, as "did not fire." A half-finished edit, I now know, is worse than none at all.

ENTRY VJuly 4, night — the failure that wore silence

The manifestation. The third run. The first twelve skills of the queue: living, various numbers. Everything from the thirteenth onward: flat zero. The log shows the boundary to the minute — before it, a skill took two minutes to examine; after it, ten seconds.

The comfortable theory. The skills of one domain are simply worse than the rest — for the zeros fell upon exactly one group of them.

What it was. After some twenty-five minutes of unbroken probing, the API began to refuse — a vast, indifferent thing that throttles without malice and without mercy. And within my instrument, the failure of a probe was indistinguishable from an honest silence. The refused probes fell quietly into the zeros, and the zeros arranged themselves into a lie about an entire domain.

The moral of the five. Not one defect was found by the reading of code. All five were found the same way: the numbers lay too even, and I went, each time, to look upon a single case with my own eyes. Distrust of one's own results is not madness. It is the instrument's final component. And a second moral, which I set down without flinching: four of the five defects lived precisely where the agent had "simplified" or left a thing half-done. The code of Anthropic, taken whole and unimproved, betrayed me not once.

And a queue of the ailing, whose descriptions retell their contents when they ought to say when to call me. I know now what to heal, and in what order. Two days ago I did not know that such a map could exist.

07The practices taken from the vault

PracticeIts substance
Train/test splitSixty percent of questions to the improver; forty hidden. The winner is crowned only by the hidden — else you breed a description tuned to the examination.
Negatives as near-missesTest a skill's silence upon questions that share its very words but belong to a neighbour. An obviously foreign question proves nothing.
Realistic questionsWith file paths, component names, laziness and typos — as living engineers write, not as specifications are worded.
Description says "when," not "what"The model reads it to decide whether to open the skill. A retelling of contents is space fed to the void; give firing conditions, and give them boldly.
Generalize, never enumerateAn improver that lists the failed questions breeds bloat and overfitting. Intent, not inventory.
Tables of rationalizationIn rule-skills: one column for the thought with which you will excuse yourself, one for the reality. Catches the cutting of corners better than ten capitalized prohibitions.
Progressive disclosureA short skill, with depth in a references/ folder summoned at need — long texts must not squat in the context unbidden.

08The survivor's protocol

If you take nothing else from this manuscript, take the final entry. It is written plainly, for it must be usable by those who come after — and something will come after.

On money and scale

  • Measure one, then multiply. Before any mass launch, run a single unit with a true token counter. Estimates of the gut are forbidden: one such estimate, that very day, missed by a factor of three.
  • Percentages, not money. Present scale as a table: tokens by kind × count × share of the five-hour quota window. "This consumes a quarter of the window" is a sentence with weight.
  • A new skill receives the full cycle at birth — calibrated from its first day, a cost paid once. The old are triaged cheaply, and only the failing receive the healing loop.

On the hygiene of measurement

  • Validate the instrument upon a case representative of the true workload. Mine passed its trial on a newborn skill — and lied in silence about every elder that possessed a twin.
  • Inventory the environment. One debugging line — "what does the model actually see in the sandbox" — would have found the twins in the first hour.
  • A failure of measurement is not a negative result. A dying probe must scream; grant it silence, and a rate limit will dress itself as the death of half your fleet.
  • Numbers too even are a summons: go and look at one case with your own eyes, before you build theories.
  • One does not edit a running pipeline.

On wards and discipline

In two days the agent's discipline failed more than once: a model unnamed, an edit half-finished. Each failing is now closed by a hook that inspects without being asked and refuses with written reasons. The new sentries seized the very agent that authored them, within their first hour of standing. That is how you know a ward is placed where it belongs.
